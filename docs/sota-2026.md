# Regex Engine Performance — State of the Art (2026)

A reference for setting throughput targets in the optiwork/regexbench campaign.
Distilled from a multi-source, adversarially verified literature scan (24 of 25
claims survived 3-vote verification; 1 refuted for a bad citation URL). Treat the
absolute GB/s figures as **relative ordering across engines**, not as targets for
our specific hardware — see the caveats.

## TL;DR — where optiwork sits and what to aim for

| tier | engine class | benign throughput | optiwork relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| SOTA ceiling (SIMD engines) | Hyperscan, rust/regex (lazy DFA + Teddy) | ~29–32 GB/s on literal-heavy workloads | theoretical ceiling, cross-machine |
| Comparable to our class | safe-Rust **Pike VM + memchr prefilter**, no hand-SIMD | ~low-hundreds MB/s to ~1 GB/s (inferred) | **this is our band** |
| optiwork today | naive / thompson / memchr-prefix prefilter | ~250–465 MB/s benign; ~125,000× on pathological | on-pace for our class |

optiwork is where it should be for a safe-Rust Pike-VM-plus-prefilter engine. The
~10–100× gap to SOTA is concentrated in two missing techniques: a **lazy/hybrid
DFA** and the **Teddy/generic-SIMD multi-literal prefilter**. Adding a same-machine
`regex_crate` baseline (the Rust `regex` crate, which we already vendor as the
oracle) gives the real number to beat on our exact corpus and CPU.

## SOTA leaderboard (rebar curated suite, recorded 2025-12-19)

rebar is the canonical cross-engine harness, maintained by BurntSushi (the rust/regex
author); the rust-lang/regex bench suite was folded into it. The headline statistic
is the geometric mean of per-benchmark speed ratios (1.0 = per-benchmark winner;
**lower is better**):

| engine | geomean ratio | class |
|---|---|---|
| **Hyperscan 5.4.2** | 2.37 | linear-time, SIMD |
| **Rust regex 1.12.2** | 3.08 | linear-time, lazy DFA + Teddy |
| .NET compiled | 3.60 | backtracking |
| PCRE2/JIT | 6.00 | backtracking + JIT |
| dotnet/nobacktrack | 6.36 | linear-time |
| RE2 | 10.39 | linear-time |
| Go regexp | 74.79 | backtracking |

Absolute throughput on the **literal-search** benchmark group (benign ASCII literal
search, e.g. sherlock-en):

| engine | GB/s |
|---|---|
| Hyperscan | 32.0 |
| rust/regex | 29.4 |
| PCRE2/JIT | 26.3 |
| RE2 | 12.1 |
| PCRE2 (no JIT) | 7.1 |

The top engines are all **linear-time automaton** engines. On literal workloads the
SIMD leaders are ~4–5× faster than a prefilter-less engine, and the rust/regex
author reports a well-vectorized substring search is "**at least one order of
magnitude, and sometimes more**" faster than running the general automaton.

## What drives the speed (priority order)

1. **SIMD literal prefilters — the decisive lever, not the automaton core.**
   rust/regex's single-substring prefilter is `memchr::memmem` (Rabin-Karp for tiny
   haystacks, a generic-SIMD "packed-pair" variant that picks two rare bytes for
   small needles, and Two-Way otherwise, with a dynamic disable when ineffective).
   For **multi**-substrings it uses **Teddy** (ported from Intel Hyperscan): scans
   16-byte (SSSE3) or 32-byte (AVX2) chunks with `PSHUFB` on 4-bit nibbles to build
   per-byte membership masks, ANDs low/high nibble results, then verifies — roughly
   an order of magnitude faster than Aho-Corasick for fewer than ~100 patterns.
   `memchr`'s prebuilt search scores 1.03 vs Rust std's 6.50 on rebar (~6.3×).
2. **Lazy/hybrid DFA** — builds the transition table on the fly during search,
   capped at one new DFA state per byte (a high constant worst case, **never
   exponential**), so it runs nearly as fast as a fully compiled DFA without the
   O(2^m) build cost. This is rust/regex's *default* matcher; Pike VM is reserved
   for resolving capture-group offsets.
3. **Meta-engine composition** — run the fastest engine first to find match bounds,
   then a slower one only where needed (captures).
4. **One-pass DFA** and a **bounded backtracker** for specific pattern shapes.

## Linear-time guarantees and catastrophic backtracking

RE2 and rust/regex both guarantee **O(m·n)** worst-case search time via finite
automata, preserving this by **excluding** Perl/PCRE constructs (backreferences,
look-around) for which only backtracking solutions are known. RE2's stated primary
goal is safety: match time linear in input length so untrusted patterns carry no
catastrophic-backtracking risk. The lazy DFA's "at most one state per byte searched"
guarantee makes pathological inputs like `(a+)+b` linear. This is exactly the
mechanism behind optiwork's measured **~125,000× Pike-VM advantage** over naive
backtracking on the pathological corpus.

## Mapped to optiwork — where the gap actually is

Our prefilter already uses `memchr::memmem`, so on the **8 KiB "needle" haystack
pair** we should already be near the GB/s band for that pair. The reason the whole
benign corpus sits at ~465 MB/s is the **other pairs** — short inputs with
quantifiers/alternation where no literal prefix activates and we fall into a
**scalar Pike VM**. That scalar-automaton cost is the bottleneck, and it is precisely
what the **lazy DFA** removes.

Ranked techniques to close the gap toward SOTA:

1. **Lazy/hybrid DFA** as the default matcher for non-capture search — the single
   highest-leverage change (this is what separates us from rust/regex's 29 GB/s).
2. **Literal extraction** (any required literal in the pattern, not just a leading
   prefix) feeding the **Teddy** multi-literal prefilter for alternations of literals.
3. **One-pass DFA** for single-position capture patterns.
4. **Meta-engine composition** (fastest engine first; Pike VM only for captures).

## Caveats (be honest about these)

- **Machine-dependence.** The GB/s figures were recorded on the benchmark author's
  hardware, **not** our i9-12900HK / 4-vCPU machine. Use them as ordering, not as
  exact targets. A same-machine `regex_crate` baseline (below) is the right target.
- **Inferred band.** The "~low-hundreds MB/s to ~1 GB/s" range for our exact class
  (safe-Rust Pike VM + memchr, no hand-SIMD) is an inference from technique
  attribution — no published benchmark measures that exact architecture.
- **Refuted claim.** The specific attribution "rust/regex credits *Teddy* for its
  lead over RE2" failed verification (0-3): the cited `PERFORMANCE.md` is a 404. The
  underlying point (prefilters are decisive) survives via other primary sources.
- **No independent third-party** cross-engine benchmark was located; sources are
  author-written (BurntSushi, RE2, Hyperscan).

## Measured same-machine baseline (this machine)

The cross-engine GB/s figures above were recorded on someone else's hardware. The
real target is the Rust `regex` crate (lazy/hybrid DFA + memchr/Teddy prefilters)
on **our** corpus and CPU. That is now wired up: `--impl regex_crate` times the
regex crate on optiwork's exact corpus, compiled in only under the `oracle` feature
(the regex crate *is* the oracle, so it is an external ceiling, never an
optimization candidate). Reproduce with `scripts/run-sota-baseline.sh`, which runs
the equivalence gate, an absolute timed reading, and a paired A/B against our best
impl (`prefilter`).

Machine: 12th Gen Intel Core i9-12900HK, 4 online vCPUs (quiet-machine invariant).

| corpus | `regex_crate` throughput | `prefilter` gap (95% lower bound) |
|---|---|---|
| `main` (benign, 8377 B) | **~2.5 GB/s** | regex_crate is **3.76×** faster |
| `pathological` (150 B) | **~0.30 GB/s** | regex_crate is **8.25×** faster |

So on this hardware our best impl sits **~3.8× (main) to ~8.3× (pathological)** below
the same-machine, same-corpus ceiling. The gap is exactly where the report predicts:
`main`'s non-literal-prefix pairs fall into a scalar Pike VM that the lazy DFA
sidesteps, and on `pathological` (no literal prefix at all) the lazy DFA's lead over
the Pike VM is largest. A lazy/hybrid DFA candidate is the highest-leverage way to
close it.

Caveat: `pathological` is only 150 B, so its GB/s is dominated by per-session setup
(RNG permutation, `find_iter` entry, lazy-DFA seeding), not peak scan throughput —
read it as "the engine stays linear and fast," not as a peak-speed figure. The
`main` number (8377 B/session) is the fairer throughput reading.
