# Regex Engine Performance — State of the Art (2026)

A reference for setting throughput targets in the optiwork/regexbench campaign.
Distilled from a multi-source, adversarially verified literature scan (24 of 25
claims survived 3-vote verification; 1 refuted for a bad citation URL). Treat the
absolute GB/s figures as **relative ordering across engines**, not as targets for
our specific hardware — see the caveats.

## TL;DR — where optiwork sits and what to aim for

| tier | engine class | benign throughput | optiwork relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| SOTA ceiling (SIMD engines) | Hyperscan, rust/regex (lazy DFA + Teddy) | ~29–32 GB/s on literal-heavy workloads | theoretical ceiling, cross-machine |
| Comparable to our class | safe-Rust **Pike VM + memchr prefilter**, no hand-SIMD | ~low-hundreds MB/s to ~1 GB/s (inferred) | **this is our band** |
| optiwork today | naive / thompson / memchr-prefix prefilter | ~250–465 MB/s benign; ~125,000× on pathological | on-pace for our class |

optiwork is where it should be for a safe-Rust Pike-VM-plus-prefilter engine. The
~10–100× gap to SOTA is concentrated in two missing techniques: a **lazy/hybrid
DFA** and the **Teddy/generic-SIMD multi-literal prefilter**. Adding a same-machine
`regex_crate` baseline (the Rust `regex` crate, which we already vendor as the
oracle) gives the real number to beat on our exact corpus and CPU.

## SOTA leaderboard (rebar curated suite, recorded 2025-12-19)

rebar is the canonical cross-engine harness, maintained by BurntSushi (the rust/regex
author); the rust-lang/regex bench suite was folded into it. The headline statistic
is the geometric mean of per-benchmark speed ratios (1.0 = per-benchmark winner;
**lower is better**):

| engine | geomean ratio | class |
|---|---|---|
| **Hyperscan 5.4.2** | 2.37 | linear-time, SIMD |
| **Rust regex 1.12.2** | 3.08 | linear-time, lazy DFA + Teddy |
| .NET compiled | 3.60 | backtracking |
| PCRE2/JIT | 6.00 | backtracking + JIT |
| dotnet/nobacktrack | 6.36 | linear-time |
| RE2 | 10.39 | linear-time |
| Go regexp | 74.79 | backtracking |

Absolute throughput on the **literal-search** benchmark group (benign ASCII literal
search, e.g. sherlock-en):

| engine | GB/s |
|---|---|
| Hyperscan | 32.0 |
| rust/regex | 29.4 |
| PCRE2/JIT | 26.3 |
| RE2 | 12.1 |
| PCRE2 (no JIT) | 7.1 |

The top engines are all **linear-time automaton** engines. On literal workloads the
SIMD leaders are ~4–5× faster than a prefilter-less engine, and the rust/regex
author reports a well-vectorized substring search is "**at least one order of
magnitude, and sometimes more**" faster than running the general automaton.

## What drives the speed (priority order)

1. **SIMD literal prefilters — the decisive lever, not the automaton core.**
   rust/regex's single-substring prefilter is `memchr::memmem` (Rabin-Karp for tiny
   haystacks, a generic-SIMD "packed-pair" variant that picks two rare bytes for
   small needles, and Two-Way otherwise, with a dynamic disable when ineffective).
   For **multi**-substrings it uses **Teddy** (ported from Intel Hyperscan): scans
   16-byte (SSSE3) or 32-byte (AVX2) chunks with `PSHUFB` on 4-bit nibbles to build
   per-byte membership masks, ANDs low/high nibble results, then verifies — roughly
   an order of magnitude faster than Aho-Corasick for fewer than ~100 patterns.
   `memchr`'s prebuilt search scores 1.03 vs Rust std's 6.50 on rebar (~6.3×).
2. **Lazy/hybrid DFA** — builds the transition table on the fly during search,
   capped at one new DFA state per byte (a high constant worst case, **never
   exponential**), so it runs nearly as fast as a fully compiled DFA without the
   O(2^m) build cost. This is rust/regex's *default* matcher; Pike VM is reserved
   for resolving capture-group offsets.
3. **Meta-engine composition** — run the fastest engine first to find match bounds,
   then a slower one only where needed (captures).
4. **One-pass DFA** and a **bounded backtracker** for specific pattern shapes.

## Linear-time guarantees and catastrophic backtracking

RE2 and rust/regex both guarantee **O(m·n)** worst-case search time via finite
automata, preserving this by **excluding** Perl/PCRE constructs (backreferences,
look-around) for which only backtracking solutions are known. RE2's stated primary
goal is safety: match time linear in input length so untrusted patterns carry no
catastrophic-backtracking risk. The lazy DFA's "at most one state per byte searched"
guarantee makes pathological inputs like `(a+)+b` linear. This is exactly the
mechanism behind optiwork's measured **~125,000× Pike-VM advantage** over naive
backtracking on the pathological corpus.

## Mapped to optiwork — where the gap actually is

Our prefilter already uses `memchr::memmem`, so on the **8 KiB "needle" haystack
pair** we should already be near the GB/s band for that pair. The reason the whole
benign corpus sits at ~465 MB/s is the **other pairs** — short inputs with
quantifiers/alternation where no literal prefix activates and we fall into a
**scalar Pike VM**. That scalar-automaton cost is the bottleneck, and it is precisely
what the **lazy DFA** removes.

Ranked techniques to close the gap toward SOTA:

1. **Lazy/hybrid DFA** as the default matcher for non-capture search — the single
   highest-leverage change (this is what separates us from rust/regex's 29 GB/s).
2. **Literal extraction** (any required literal in the pattern, not just a leading
   prefix) feeding the **Teddy** multi-literal prefilter for alternations of literals.
3. **One-pass DFA** for single-position capture patterns.
4. **Meta-engine composition** (fastest engine first; Pike VM only for captures).

## What we tried: a lazy/hybrid DFA candidate (rejected twice, promoted on the third)

Acting on the ranking above, we implemented a `lazy_dfa` candidate two ways and ran both
through the full campaign (frozen two-corpus rule). Both were byte-identical to the
oracle (equivalence gate passed on `main` and `pathological`; a 20,000-case differential
property test vs `thompson`) and both were **rejected** — slower than the champion
`prefilter` on both corpora:

| implementation | `main` LB ratio | `pathological` LB ratio | verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| cached-closure Pike VM | 0.16 (−83%) | 0.79 (−8.5%) | not_promoted |
| true O(1)/byte transition-caching DFA + cross-call cache | 0.034 (−96.5%) | 0.54 (−42%) | not_promoted |

The full DFA was *worse*, not better.

### Corrected diagnosis (measured 2026-07-23)

The first explanation we wrote — "`main` is a `memchr` wall, so no scalar automaton can
win it" — was a **misdiagnosis**. It confused *byte-share* with *time-share*. A direct
split-timing of `prefilter` on `main` (compile-once, DFA-warmed microbench) shows where
the time actually goes:

| segment | bytes | share of `prefilter`'s `main` **time** |
|---|---|---|
| the 8 KiB "needle" pair (literal, memchr) | 8192 (97.8%) | **~9%** |
| the 42 small non-literal "tail" pairs (scalar Pike VM) | 185 (2.2%) | **~90%** |

`prefilter` and `thompson` spend the same ~10 µs on the tail (the prefilter just
delegates non-literal patterns to the Pike VM), and `regex_crate` does that same tail in
~1.9 µs — its lazy DFA is **~5.5× faster than our scalar Pike VM there**. That single
185-byte segment is the entire 3.8× campaign gap on `main`. The needle is a `memchr`
*tie* in time (both engines vectorize it); it only dominates the **byte** count, and
throughput is reported as bytes/time.

So the real reason both `lazy_dfa` attempts lost was not "a DFA can't beat memchr." It
was two **fixable** implementation defects:

- **No literal fast-path.** The candidate ran its scalar DFA over the 8192-byte needle.
  The needle is only ~9% of `prefilter`'s time but **97.8% of its bytes**, and the
  campaign metric is bytes/time — so scanning 8 KiB scalarly (~5.5 µs) instead of with
  `memchr` (~1 µs) sank the GB/s figure by itself. Any candidate that does not keep the
  `memchr` path for literal-prefix patterns cannot win the `main` metric, no matter how
  good its automaton is.
- **Per-call rebuild + `HashMap` transitions.** On the tail's ~4-byte inputs, `thompson`'s
  inline Pike VM (no hashing, no per-call setup) beat DFA machinery built per call. A
  measured breakdown shows per-call `Program::new` is ~33% of the tail's time; the rest
  is the scalar thread-walk that a *cached* flat-table DFA removes. The cross-call cache
  that would amortize the DFA build across the campaign's sessions was never effective.

### The real lever: a meta-engine, not Teddy

The fix is exactly `regex_crate`'s architecture, built in-house: **keep the `memchr`
prefilter for literal-prefix patterns** (the needle stays at ~1 µs) **and add a
flat-array lazy DFA with a persistent cross-call cache for non-literal patterns** — which
attacks the ~90% tail on `main` and 100% of `pathological` (where `regex_crate` is 8.17×
ahead). A plain compile-cache captures only the ~33% rebuild cost; the flat cached DFA is
needed for the remaining ~3.5× on the tail. (Teddy, the multi-literal SIMD prefilter, is
**not** the lever for these corpora — there are no multi-literal alternation pairs; it
would help only on a new corpus with such patterns.)

### Result (2026-07-23): the meta-engine promoted

Acting on the diagnosis above, a third `lazy_dfa` was built exactly as described —
`memchr` kept for literal-prefix patterns, a flat-table lazy DFA with a persistent
cross-call cache for non-literal patterns, the leftmost-first start recovered via cached
provenance, and an allocation-free hot path (flat transition/provenance tables, a
memoized seed-merge, and a clone-free cache-hit lookup). It was byte-identical to the
oracle (equivalence gate on both corpora; 20,000-case differential property test vs
`thompson`) and ran through the full frozen campaign:

| implementation | `main` LB ratio | `pathological` LB ratio | verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| v1 cached-closure Pike VM | 0.16 (−83%) | 0.79 (−8.5%) | not_promoted |
| v2 true O(1)/byte DFA + cross-call cache | 0.034 (−96.5%) | 0.54 (−42%) | not_promoted |
| **v3 flat meta-engine DFA (memchr + cached DFA)** | **1.015 (+7.2%)** | **1.732 (+77.2%)** | **promoted → confirmed** |

`lazy_dfa` is the first candidate to beat `prefilter` on **both** corpora, so it is the
new accepted baseline. The headline numbers (paired A/B vs the prior champion
`prefilter`, 95% one-sided lower bound):

- **`main`**: lower bound **1.015×** (point +7.2%). The win is the ~90% non-literal tail,
  where the cached flat DFA beats the scalar Pike VM; the margin is modest because the
  literal needle is routed back through `prefilter` and pays a little per-call overhead.
- **`pathological`**: lower bound **1.732×** (point +77.2%). No literals here, so the DFA
  runs end-to-end and the cached transitions dominate the catastrophic thread-walk.
- Held-out confirmation (fresh seed vs the **original** `naive` baseline): `main` lower
  bound 2.345×, `pathological` 208 382×. Confirmed.

Net effect on the gap to the same-machine `regex_crate` ceiling: the champion moved from
`prefilter` to `lazy_dfa`, recovering the non-literal-tail and pathological portions of
the gap. The remaining gap is concentrated where this architecture cannot help on these
corpora — the literal needle (already at `memchr`) and the tiny per-call overhead on a
2.2%-of-bytes tail. Reproduce with `bash scripts/run-campaign.sh
target/runs/regex-demo-lazydfa-v3`.

### Result (2026-07-23): `flat_dfa` — the per-call constant-factor fold

The same diagnosis that located the win for `lazy_dfa` also located its ceiling. The
tiny non-literal-tail pairs (≤100 B, ~2.2% of `main`'s bytes but ~81% of `lazy_dfa`'s
`main` time) pay a per-call constant factor — routing AST walks, a cache hash, and
per-call scratch `Vec` allocations — not a per-byte DFA cost. A tail-breakdown
microbench put `lazy_dfa` at only ~0.84× of `thompson` on those pairs; the regex
crate runs them at ~46 ns/pair vs our ~200 ns/pair. That gap is a constant factor,
so the lever is to fold the per-call work, not to change the DFA walk.

`flat_dfa` is the same lazy/hybrid DFA core as `lazy_dfa`, byte-identical to the
oracle, with the per-call fold: the `literal_prefix`/`anchored` routing verdicts are
computed once at build and cached on the `Dfa`, so a cache hit routes without
re-walking the AST; the cache probe is a single `get_mut` (one hash, no `String`
clone on hit); and `cached_find`'s two scratch `Vec`s are lifted to reusable `Dfa`
fields via `std::mem::take`/restore (no per-call allocation). It passed the
equivalence gate on both corpora and the 20,000-case differential property test vs
`thompson`, then ran the full frozen campaign:

| implementation | `main` LB ratio | `pathological` LB ratio | verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| **flat_dfa (per-call fold, same DFA core)** | **1.202 (+28.5%)** | **1.214 (+25.8%)** | **promoted → confirmed** |

`flat_dfa` is the first candidate to beat `lazy_dfa` on **both** corpora (campaign
baseline transitions: `naive` → `prefilter` → `lazy_dfa` → `flat_dfa`), so it is the
new accepted baseline. Held-out confirmation (fresh seed vs the **original** `naive`
baseline): `main` lower bound 3.066×, `pathological` 294 708×. Confirmed. Reproduce
with `bash scripts/run-campaign.sh target/runs/regex-demo-flatdfa`.

Net effect on the gap to the same-machine `regex_crate` ceiling: the champion moved
from `lazy_dfa` to `flat_dfa`, narrowing the gap on both corpora (progression table
in the same-machine baseline section below). What remains is structural on these
corpora — the literal needle is already at `memchr`, and the residual tail gap is the
few constant-factor moves the per-call fold did not remove, plus the regex crate's
hand-SIMD scan path (we are pure safe Rust, `#![forbid(unsafe_code)]`).

## Caveats (be honest about these)

- **Machine-dependence.** The GB/s figures were recorded on the benchmark author's
  hardware, **not** our i9-12900HK / 4-vCPU machine. Use them as ordering, not as
  exact targets. A same-machine `regex_crate` baseline (below) is the right target.
- **Inferred band.** The "~low-hundreds MB/s to ~1 GB/s" range for our exact class
  (safe-Rust Pike VM + memchr, no hand-SIMD) is an inference from technique
  attribution — no published benchmark measures that exact architecture.
- **Refuted claim.** The specific attribution "rust/regex credits *Teddy* for its
  lead over RE2" failed verification (0-3): the cited `PERFORMANCE.md` is a 404. The
  underlying point (prefilters are decisive) survives via other primary sources.
- **No independent third-party** cross-engine benchmark was located; sources are
  author-written (BurntSushi, RE2, Hyperscan).

## Measured same-machine baseline (this machine)

The cross-engine GB/s figures above were recorded on someone else's hardware. The
real target is the Rust `regex` crate (lazy/hybrid DFA + memchr/Teddy prefilters)
on **our** corpus and CPU. That is wired up: `--impl regex_crate` times the regex
crate on optiwork's exact corpus, compiled in only under the `oracle` feature (the
regex crate *is* the oracle, so it is an external ceiling, never an optimization
candidate). Reproduce with `scripts/run-sota-baseline.sh`, which runs the
equivalence gate, absolute timed readings for both engines, and a paired A/B of our
accepted baseline (`flat_dfa`, the current champion) vs `regex_crate`.

Machine: 12th Gen Intel Core i9-12900HK, 4 online vCPUs (quiet-machine invariant;
system load ~0.6 at measurement time). Numbers below were measured 2026-07-22–23,
all the same way (paired A/B vs `regex_crate`, 16 blocks, seed 42; `main`
sessions=200, `pathological` sessions=5000). Equivalence gate passed on both corpora
(`regex_crate` == oracle): 43 pairs on `main`, 7 pairs on `pathological`.

`regex_crate` sustained throughput (the ceiling engine): **2.38 GB/s** on `main`
(8377 B), **0.30 GB/s** on `pathological` (150 B).

The gap to that ceiling, by champion (95% one-sided lower bound, `regex_crate` ÷
champion):

| champion | `main` gap | `pathological` gap | notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| `prefilter` | 3.72× | 7.98× | memchr literal needle; scalar Pike VM on the rest |
| `lazy_dfa` | 3.36× | 4.54× | cached flat DFA on non-literal pairs; per-call constant factor on the tiny tail |
| `flat_dfa` (current) | 2.53× | 3.65× | same DFA core + the per-call fold |

Each promotion narrows the gap on **both** corpora monotonically. `flat_dfa` is the
closest to the ceiling of any optiwork champion: **~2.5× below on `main`, ~3.7×
below on `pathological`** by the 95% one-sided lower bound. The remaining gap is
where this architecture cannot help on these corpora — the literal needle is already
at `memchr`, and the residual is the few per-call constant-factor moves the fold did
not remove, plus the regex crate's hand-SIMD scan path (we are pure safe Rust,
`#![forbid(unsafe_code)]`).

Two measurement shapes are in play here and must not be cross-divided:

- The **sustained GB/s** is `regex_crate` alone at high session counts (sessions=2000
  on `main`, 100000 on `pathological`), which amortizes per-session setup (RNG
  permutation, lazy-DFA seeding) to expose near-peak scan throughput.
- The **gap ratios** come from the paired A/B (sessions=200 on `main`, 5000 on
  `pathological`, 16 blocks) — the apples-to-apples comparison the campaign uses.

Dividing the sustained GB/s by a gap ratio does **not** yield a champion's
throughput, because the two are different shapes (different session counts mean
different per-session-setup amortization). The ratio is the authoritative gap; the
GB/s is a "how fast is the ceiling engine" reference.

Caveat: `pathological` is only 150 B, so its GB/s is dominated by per-session setup
(RNG permutation, `find_iter` entry, lazy-DFA seeding), not peak scan throughput —
read it as "the engine stays linear and fast," not as a peak-speed figure. The
`main` number (8377 B/session) is the fairer throughput reading.
